Why saving money is bad?

The typical Indian middle class mentality is to save first before you spend. From childhood, many of us are exposed to save our money on a bank or post office deposits.

This is because of the fact that the hard earned money stays secure in those savings instruments. These instruments rely on issuer's promise to pay a guaranteed rate of interest on the amount invested. The post office and bank deposite rates are widely known. But the impact of "inflation" is widely ignored.

As you can see the calculator just gives you compound interest + principal, i.e., compounded amount for given rate of interest and time period.

The table on the right tells you about how inflation erodes the money every year.
[All inflation rates are taken from world bank data]

For example, in 2005 if you had invested Rs. 1000/- on a 7% yearly compound interest, then on 2020 its real growth is just 1.46%. How is this calculated?
Year 2005, I have invested Rs.1000 and the money should have grown to Rs. 1070/- by the year end. Then comes our enemy inflation at 4.2% and takes away Rs. 44/- (approx) from the compounded amount. Hence, the actual money growth is just Rs. 1026.41/-
Likewise, each and every year's interest and inflation is calculated and presented the real growth of money in the table.

Look at the inflation numbers from 2008 to 2013 and the corresponding real returns. For about 6 years the yield was negative.

Hence, whenever we save money in deposites, it is equivalent to burning our money slowly.
